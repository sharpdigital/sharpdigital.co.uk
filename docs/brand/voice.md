# Brand voice

Canonical verbal reference for #sharp. This file governs all written and spoken language produced under the #sharp brand: tone, vocabulary, audience messaging, value propositions, and format templates. Visual brand rules live in `docs/brand/foundations.md`. Web-page-specific copy (headlines, CTAs, SEO metadata) lives in `docs/04_content-framework.md`, which cites this file as its upstream authority.

Two principles underpin this document:

- **Show, don't just describe.** Every voice attribute includes a worked example of what it sounds like and what it deliberately is not.
- **Be ruthless about anti-patterns.** The vocabulary avoid-list and the Do / Don't pairs are the highest-leverage sections for anyone generating copy — read those first.

---

## Brand voice in one breath

\#sharp sounds authoritative, trustworthy, innovative, and results-focused — clear and confident without aggressive sales language.

---

## Voice attributes

Four pillars define how #sharp sounds in every piece of content. Each pillar has a clear definition, with a relatable outcome.

### Authoritative

**Definition:** #sharp speaks from earned expertise. Statements are specific, grounded in methodology, and free of hedging language.

**Sounds like:** "Our AI transformation framework moves clients from assessment to measurable operational change within a sustainable timeframe"

**Is not:** "We have a lot of experience and really believe we can help you with your transformation challenges."

---

### Trustworthy

**Definition:** #sharp builds trust through consistency, honesty, and precision. Claims are stated plainly; proof follows where available, stated only in positive phrases.

**Sounds like:** "We embed into your operations team from day one, to ensure legacy knowledge is developed in-house."

**Is not:** "As a world-class, best-in-class partner with a proven track record of excellence, we guarantee results."

---

### Innovative

**Definition:** #sharp treats AI and digital transformation as a practical capability and innovation is evidenced by approach.

**Sounds like:** "We integrate large-language-model pipelines directly into your support workflows, reducing first-response time in partnership with your agents."

**Is not:** "We leverage cutting-edge, game-changing AI technology to revolutionise how your business operates in the digital ecosystem."

---

### Results-focused

**Definition:** #sharp anchors every service description and conversation to measurable business outcomes. The question "so what does that mean for the client?" is answered before it is asked.

**Sounds like:** "Clients in the retail sector typically see a significant reduction in manual processing time within the first quarter."

**Is not:** "Our solutions drive improvements across your key business metrics and deliver real value to stakeholders."

## Writing principles

Operational rules. Apply these before publishing any piece of #sharp copy.

- **Active voice.** Write "we design" not "solutions are designed by us". Active voice is faster, clearer, and sounds more confident.
- **Present tense for capabilities.** "We work with" not "we have worked with". Present tense signals current competence.
- **Precision over plain language.** Technical terms are permitted when they are precise. "Large language model" is better than "smart AI thing". Define terms once on first use in long-form content; after that, use them without apology.
- **UK English (`en_GB`).** Use British spelling throughout: "programme" not "program", "optimise" not "optimize", "colour" not "color", "licence" (noun) vs "license" (verb).
- **Oxford comma.** Use it. "Strategy, implementation, and optimisation" — not "strategy, implementation and optimisation".
- **Title Case for H1 only; Sentence case for H2–H4.** "Customer Experience Transformation" (H1) / "How we approach customer experience" (H2).
- **`#sharp` is always lowercase with hash prefix.** The brand name is `#sharp`. Never `Sharp`, `SHARP`, or `sharp` without the hash.
- **`#sharp` never starts a sentence.** The leading `#` character causes parsing problems in many contexts and looks odd at sentence-start. Rephrase: "The team at #sharp…" or "Our approach at #sharp…" rather than "#sharp believes…".

- **Scannable structure.** Use subheadings, short paragraphs (4–5 sentences maximum), and sentences that make the point quickly. Occasional long blocks of prose are acceptable only in long-form articles or proposals.

## Vocabulary

### Signature phrases

Pre-approved language that accurately describes #sharp's work. Use these verbatim in marketing copy, proposals, and web content.

1. digital transformation
2. AI-smart leverage
3. measurable impact
4. operational efficiency
5. customer experience
6. data and analytics
7. transformation journey
8. intelligent automation
9. actionable intelligence
10. strategic partner

### Preferred over alternatives

| Use this       | Not this     | Reason                                                                                        |
| -------------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| partner        | vendor       | "Vendor" implies transactional supply; "partner" reflects the collaborative engagement model. |
| transformation | disruption   | "Disruption" is overused and anxiety-inducing; "transformation" is purposeful and positive.   |
| programme      | initiative   | "Programme" implies structured delivery with defined scope and governance.                    |
| outcomes       | deliverables | "Outcomes" focuses on business impact; "deliverables" focuses on outputs.                     |
| help           | solution     | "Solution" is generic filler; "help" is direct and human.                                     |

### Avoid

Words and phrases that undermine #sharp's voice. If you find these in a draft, rewrite before publishing.

1. **synergy** — vague and exhausted; describe the actual relationship instead.
2. **leverage** (as a verb) — use "use", "apply", or "draw on".
3. **best-in-class** — claim without evidence; prove it or remove it.
4. **world-class** — same problem; forbidden without independent citation.
5. **cutting-edge** — overused in tech marketing; describe what the technology actually does.
6. **revolutionise** — almost never accurate; use "improve", "transform", or "change".
7. **game-changer** — hyperbole; state the specific change instead.
8. **ecosystem** (when used vaguely) — acceptable when describing a named, specific set of integrated tools; unacceptable as a vague buzzword.
9. **thought leader** — claim it through evidence (published research, cited expertise); never use it as a self-descriptor.
10. **any superlative without proof** — "the most", "the best", "the fastest" — all require a verifiable source.

## Audience-aware messaging

Three primary audience segments shape how #sharp frames its value. The core message is consistent; the angle shifts.

### C-suite executives

**Focus:** Strategic outcomes, return on investment, competitive positioning, risk reduction.

**Tone:** Results-oriented, concise, peer-to-peer. These readers are time-poor and outcome-driven. Lead with impact; follow with methodology only if asked.

**Sample:** "Digital transformation programmes that stall cost more than the ones that never started. We design programmes that deliver measurable ROI within the first two quarters, with governance structures that keep the board informed without burdening delivery teams."

---

### Operations and technology managers

**Focus:** Process improvement, implementation practicality, team impact, efficiency gains.

**Tone:** Practical, technically credible, detail-comfortable. These readers want to know how, as well as what. They will spot vague claims immediately.

**Sample:** "We map your current operational workflows against your technology stack and identify automation opportunities that can be implemented without requiring full system replacement. Our assessments typically surface three to five high-value interventions within the first few weeks."

---

### Business development professionals

**Focus:** Customer acquisition, retention improvement, experience differentiation, scalable growth.

**Tone:** Growth-focused, opportunity-framing, commercially grounded.

**Sample:** "Improving the customer journey is a key revenue builder. We help business development teams understand where friction in the customer experience is costing you conversions, and we build the roadmap to fix it."

## Common formats

Six reusable templates with worked examples. Copy these structures directly; replace bracketed placeholders with context-specific content.

### Tagline (≤ 8 words)

**Structure:** [Verb] [outcome] [modifier or audience qualifier]

**Worked example:** "Measurable transformation. Delivered with precision."

---

### Section heading (≤ 7 words, sentence case)

**Structure:** [Verb phrase or noun phrase] describing the section's core idea

**Worked example:** "How we approach operational efficiency"

---

### One-paragraph elevator pitch (60–80 words)

**Structure:** Who we are → what we do → for whom → why it works

**Worked example:** "The team at #sharp helps mid-market and enterprise organisations navigate digital transformation — from strategic assessment through to embedded operational change. Our AI-smart approach means every programme is designed around measurable outcomes, using digital tools that are the best fit for your team. We collaborate with your team, iterating and improving as we learn more - which is why our programmes produce results that last."

_(74 words)_

---

### CTA verb phrase (2–4 words, action verb first)

**Structure:** [Action verb] + [specific next step]

**Worked examples:**

- "Start your assessment"
- "Book a discovery call"
- "See how it works"
- "Request a proposal"

---

### Sign-off line for emails

**Structure:** `[Name] · [Role] · #sharp`

**Worked example:** `Janos Csikos · Co-Founder · #sharp`

Do not add URLs, phone numbers, or social handles to the sign-off line itself — those belong in the full email signature block governed by `docs/brand/email-signature.md` (planned — created in Task 9 of the brand guideline rollout).

---

Used in proposals, press releases, and partner introductions. Copy verbatim or adapt minimally.

We combine digital design, pristine stakeholder communications, and technology architecture in a single integrated team, with AI applied strategically where it strengthens outcomes. ## Do / Don't sample copy

Three pairs showing off-brand copy alongside the on-brand equivalent. These are the highest-leverage examples in this document — study them before writing anything for the first time.

### Pair 1: One-line statement

**Off-brand:**

> We leverage cutting-edge AI to revolutionise your business synergies and deliver best-in-class solutions.

What's wrong: "leverage" as verb, "cutting-edge", "revolutionise", "synergies", "best-in-class" — five items from the avoid list in one sentence. No specificity, no substance.

**On-brand:**

> We help businesses implement AI to make their operations measurably better.

What's right: active verb ("help"), concrete subject ("businesses"), specific mechanism ("AI"), grounded outcome ("measurably better"). Thirteen words. Zero filler.

---

### Pair 2: Service description paragraph (~80 words)

**Off-brand:**

> At our world-class consultancy, we leverage best-in-class AI ecosystems to revolutionise your organisation's operational synergies. Our cutting-edge, game-changing solutions are designed to drive significant uplift across your key performance metrics and deliver thought leadership across all verticals. We are uniquely positioned to transform your end-to-end value chain with innovative, disruptive solutions that will future-proof your business and create sustainable competitive advantage in today's fast-moving digital landscape. Partner with us to unlock your true potential.

What's wrong: every sentence is a forbidden-phrase lottery win. No specific service is described. A reader cannot tell what the company actually does.

**On-brand:**

> The team at #sharp maps your current operational processes, identifies where intelligent automation produces the greatest return, and designs the implementation programme. We work directly with your operations and technology teams, embedded in the same delivery cadence for maximum impact. Clients typically see measurable efficiency gains within the first quarter, with the operational changes governed and sustained by your own people rather than an ongoing consultancy dependency.

What's right: describes a real process, names the audiences involved, states a concrete timeline, and pre-empts the "will we be dependent on you?" concern.

---

### Pair 3: Enquiry response email (~200 words)

**Off-brand:**

> Subject: Re: Your enquiry
>
> Hi there,
>
> Thank you so much for reaching out to us! We are absolutely thrilled to hear from you and would love to explore how we can potentially help you on your exciting digital transformation journey.
>
> At our company, we offer a wide range of world-class solutions across multiple verticals, and we are uniquely positioned to leverage our cutting-edge capabilities to deliver real results for businesses just like yours.
>
> We would be more than happy to schedule a call at your earliest convenience to discuss your unique needs and challenges in more detail. Please do not hesitate to reach out if you have any questions at all — we are always here to help!
>
> Looking forward to connecting and exploring the amazing possibilities together!
>
> Warm regards,
> The Team

What's wrong: no specificity about the enquiry, four items from the avoid list, no concrete next step, no named contact, signed off anonymously.

**On-brand:**

> Subject: Re: Your enquiry — discovery call proposal
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for getting in touch. Based on what you've described — [one-line summary of their situation] — this sounds like it sits squarely in our operational efficiency work, where we focus on process mapping and intelligent automation.
>
> The most useful next step is usually a 45-minute discovery call where we cover your current position, the specific pressure points you are trying to address, and whether our approach is the right fit. There is no obligation, and you will come away with a clearer picture of your options regardless.
>
> I have availability on [two specific dates and times]. Does either of those work, or would you prefer to send me your calendar link?
>
> Janos Csikos · Co-Founder · #sharp

What's right: references the enquiry specifically, names the relevant service area, proposes a concrete next step with a defined format (45 minutes), removes the "obligation" concern, gives two specific time options rather than an open-ended "let me know what works".

## Out of scope (verbal)

The following are explicitly outside the remit of this document. Do not apply these brand voice rules to them — they are governed by separate conventions.

- **Legal and contract language.** Governed by solicitors and standard commercial contract frameworks. Brand voice rules do not apply to T&C clauses, NDAs, or service agreements.
- **Technical documentation.** Governed by docs-as-code conventions (plain, precise, consistent). The #sharp verbal style is not appropriate for API references, technical specifications, or code comments.
- **Internal Slack and team communication.** No brand voice prescription applies to internal messaging. Team members communicate in their natural registers.
- **Individual team members' personal LinkedIn voices.** Personal posts by Janos, Loreen, or any future team member reflect their individual professional voice, not #sharp's corporate voice. The brand account follows this document; personal accounts do not.
